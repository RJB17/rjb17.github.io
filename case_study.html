Case Study: Wayfinding

Introduction

Context

University group project to design and evaluate an interative system using user-centred design (UCD) methodology.

Problem Statement

Design a wayfinding application to assist people in navigating the University's campuses. The application must allow users to:

  • Search for the location of a room within a building.
  • Get directions to a room or building.
  • Tailor routes based on specified criteria.

The target users were members of the University

Role & Responsibilities

I worked in team of 4. I managed, and contributed significently to all parts of, the project.

Process

A user-centred design process was used. It was restricted by the assessment specifications. The process comprised 4 sets of activities: User Research; Personas & Scenarios; Design & Prototyping; and Usability Testing. The above diagram illustrates the activity sequencing. This case study will next dicuss each of the activities in turn.

User Research

Related Product Audit

Relevant existing services were examined to understand how they address user goals and need. The services investigated were: Google Maps; the Universty's own interactive map; and physical navigation signs around the University's campuses. This understanding was used to inform interview design.

To evaluate each service, it was identified if it implemented each of the functions our design was required to and how usable the implementation was. Usability was evaluated by considering how well it satisfied usability goals. The evaluation could have been improved by instead using an expert evaluation method.

Persona Hypothesis

A personas hypothesis (PH) was created to inform interview planning. The PH predicts who the users are and their needs and goals. It is based on both demographic information and behavoural patterns. The PH helps identify who should be interviewed and interview topics. Additionally,it documents the designers assumptions enabling them to be critiqued.

For the target user, members of the University, the PH considered a range of behavoural patterns including:

  • Why users travel? eg. for class; to shop; to socialise.
  • When users travel? eg. during the working day; at night.
  • How do users travel? eg. walk; bus; bike.

Semi-structured Interviews

The team conducted 20 semi-structured interviews (SSI) to learn about the users and their goals. Interviews were conducted in pairs such that one member acted as the interviewer and the second as a notetaker. Google forms was used to capture the participants responses. I, and the teammate I worked with, conducted 14 interviews and alternated roles.

The assessment specification required the use of either questionniares or user interviews. SSI were chosen becuase of their flexibility. The SSI format is flexible because it allows the interviewer to probe the participant and for the format to be modified during the interview process. This flexibility enabled us to use SSI to quickly gain an indepth understanding of the target users.

The interviews were designed to be about 15 minutes long. This length was chosen because it would allow for us to recruit and interview a suitable number of participants within our time constraints. The interviews were comprised of three sections. The first section captured the participants background. The second section explored participants goals when interacting with campus. The third section investigated their current experience when interacting with campus.

If the project had been less contrained by time and the specification, a combination of methods could have been used gain a broader and more indepth understanding of users. Questionniares would have enabled reaching a larger and more diverse set of users. Combining SSI with contextual inquiry would have enaled a more indepth understanding of users existing practices and goals.
