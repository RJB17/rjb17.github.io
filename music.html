Case Study: Playlist Player

Introduction

I designed, prototyped and usability tested a music player application for touchscreen smartphones.

I did this for fun and practise.

I developed an interactive prototype using Figma. I usability tested the prototype with some participants remotely and some in-person.

Tags: prototyping, interaction design, usability testing.

Design Motivation

This design had two sources of inspiration. The first was material I had been reading about the ergonomics of smartphone interaction. The second was observations I’d made about how people listen to music.

I believe that user-centred design (UCD) processes, that use user research, are the best way to design. However, I did not use a full UCD process for this project because my motivations for this project were to practise specific skills and explore specific ideas.

Ergonomics

Typically when using a smartphone the hand is used to both hold the device and to interact with it. The thumb is used for most interactions with the screen such as tapping, dragging and swiping. These thumb interactions are constrained by the hands hold of the phone and the thumbs morphology. The thumb has limited reach and the movements to access different parts of the screen can differ notably. Consequently, the usability of the touchscreen varies across the surface of the screen.

How the usability varies has been researched. This research has resulted in design recommendations including:

  • Position inputs in the top right and middle left areas of the phone. Do not position them in top left and bottom right (Trudeau et al, 2012).
  • Position the most frequent targets in the center of the screen (Park and Han, 2010).
  • Don’t position inputs on the extremes of the functional area (Bergstrom-Lehtovirta and Oulasvirta, 2014).

The functional area refers to the area of the screen the user can reach when holding the device in their rest grip. In short, it excludes the corners of the screen.

In summary, there is a strong argument to position inputs toward the center of the screen and not the corners. Despite this, many current applications position input in the corners of the screen. I used this project to explore applying these design recommendations.

Listening to Music

People listen to songs in series. That series may be an album, a playlist or a queue - I will refer to these collectively as playlists. Current apps place a lot focus on the song being currently played. In comparison the playlist is given a minor role. I hypothesize that this prioritisation of current song and playlist is misaligned with how users interact with music.

Of course I expect that users care about what song is currently playing. I also expect that users care more about the songs previously played and a lot more about the songs soon to be played, then is reflected by current designs.

These beliefs have made me interested in designing a music application that makes the playlist the lead character.

These are just my beliefs and assumptions - it’s very possible they are completely wrong. If I were working on this for other reasons (not primarily to practise prototyping) I would be investigating this in depth by researching users.
