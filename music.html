Case Study: Playlist Player

Introduction

I designed, prototyped and usability tested a music player application for touchscreen smartphones.

I did this for fun and practise.

I developed an interactive prototype using Figma. I usability tested the prototype with some participants remotely and some in-person.

Tags: prototyping, Figma, interaction design, usability testing.

Design Motivation

This design had two sources of inspiration. The first was material I had been reading about the ergonomics of smartphone interaction. The second was observations I’d made about how people listen to music.

I believe that user-centred design (UCD) processes, that use user research, are the best way to design. However, I did not use a full UCD process for this project because my motivations for this project were to practise specific skills and explore specific ideas.

Ergonomics

Typically when using a smartphone the hand is used to both hold the device and to interact with it. The thumb is used for most interactions with the screen such as tapping, dragging and swiping. These thumb interactions are constrained by the hands hold of the phone and the thumbs morphology. The thumb has limited reach and the movements to access different parts of the screen can differ notably. Consequently, the usability of the touchscreen varies across the surface of the screen.

How the usability varies has been researched. This research has resulted in design recommendations including:

  • Position inputs in the top right and middle left areas of the phone. Do not position them in top left and bottom right (Trudeau et al, 2012).
  • Position the most frequent targets in the center of the screen (Park and Han, 2010).
  • Don’t position inputs on the extremes of the functional area (Bergstrom-Lehtovirta and Oulasvirta, 2014).

The functional area refers to the area of the screen the user can reach when holding the device in their rest grip. In short, it excludes the corners of the screen.

In summary, there is a strong argument to position inputs toward the center of the screen and not the corners. Despite this, many current applications position input in the corners of the screen. I used this project to explore applying these design recommendations.

Listening to Music

People listen to songs in series. That series may be an album, a playlist or a queue - I will refer to these collectively as playlists. Current apps place a lot focus on the song being currently played. In comparison the playlist is given a minor role. I hypothesize that this prioritisation of current song and playlist is misaligned with how users interact with music.

Of course I expect that users care about what song is currently playing. I also expect that users care more about the songs previously played and a lot more about the songs soon to be played, then is reflected by current designs.

These beliefs have made me interested in designing a music application that makes the playlist the lead character.

These are just my beliefs and assumptions - it’s very possible they are completely wrong. If I were working on this for other reasons (not primarily to practise prototyping) I would be investigating this in depth by researching users.

Design & Prototyping

Approach

I wanted the playlist to be the core of this application so I started the design by considering how I could visually represent it. I first explored ideas by making rough paper sketches of screen layouts and considering how they could be manipulated to introduce functionality. When doing so I was trying to apply the ergonomics motivated design recommendations by positioning the interactions in the central area of the screen. Once I had settled on a general layout I moved to Figma to develop the next iterations.

Design

The main screen of the application is ‘Playing Playlist Screen’ shown below. The playlist is the focus of the application. The user is able to see and interact with: the songs previously played, the current song and the songs next in the playlist.

Layout

The ‘Playing Playlist’ screen has a two column layout as illustrated below. The first column (blue) spans the majority of the screen and is positioned to the left of the screen; this column is used as a container to hold the contents of the playlist. The column is also scrollable thus is able to accomodate playlists of variable size. The column to the right (purple) contains playlist controls including play, pause and shuffle. The right column is fixed in position.

I chose this layout because it is well aligned with the design recommendations as it enables almost all interactions to be positioned in the vertically central region.

Two Column Layout

Playlist Manipulation

Three important playlist functions I required the design to feature were:

  • Song adding
  • Song removal
  • Song reordering

The design enables these actions largely using direct manipulation. The user is able to insert songs by holding on the position in the playlist where they want to place the song. Songs can be dragged around to reorder them or dragged out of the list to remove them.

Usability Testing

Description

To test the design and learn about how it could be improved I decided to run a few think aloud usability tests. I ran two tests remotely and one in-person.

For the remote tests I set up a video call with the participant such that they were able to view their screen and I shared the Figma prototype with them. This enabled the participant to interact with the prototype, me to observe them and us to converse. A limitation of this testing was that they interacted with a simulation of a phone using a laptop rather than a physical phone. Consequently the validity of the test with respect to ergonomics is low. For the in-person test, the participant interacted with the prototype on a phone using the Figma mirror app.

Findings

Participants struggled to discover the interactions for adding and removing songs. This is not too surprising because there is nothing to visually indicate their existence. Once the participants had experienced the interactions they appeared happy to use them going forward. This usability problem could potentially be addressed by either adding signifiers to the design or adding a tutorial flow to introduce them to new users. Other than this, participants did not encounter issues interacting with the prototype.
