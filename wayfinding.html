Case Study: Wayfinding

Introduction

University group project to design and evaluate an interactive wayfinding application using user-centred design (UCD) methodology.

I worked in a team of 4. I contributed significantly to all parts of the project.

The project was the assessment for a user-centred design module studied as part of a MSc in Human-centred Interactive Technologies.

Tags: user-centred design (UCD), user research, personas & scenarios, design & prototyping, usability testing.

Problem Statement

Design a wayfinding application to assist people in navigating the University's campuses.

The application must allow users to:

  • Search for the location of a room within a building.
  • Get directions to a room or building.
  • Tailor routes based on specified criteria.

The target users were members of the University.

Process

A user-centred design process was used. It was restricted by the assessment specifications to involve certain activities. The process comprised 4 sets of activities: user research, personas & scenarios, design & prototyping and usability testing. The above diagram illustrates the sequence of activities. Design & prototyping has a cyclic relationship with usability testing because usability testing was used to inform modifications of the design. This case study will next discuss each of the activities in turn.

User Research

The foundation of good user-centred design is good user research. For this project the main user research activity was semi-structured interviews. The interviews were informed and complemented by an audit of related products and the creation of a persona hypothesis.

Related Product Audit

Relevant existing services were examined to understand how they address user goals and needs. The services investigated were: Google Maps, the University's own interactive map, and physical navigation signs around the University's campuses.

To evaluate each service, it was identified if it implemented each of the functions our design was required to and how usable the implementation was. Usability was evaluated by considering how well it achieved usability goals. The evaluation could have been improved by instead using an expert evaluation method.

Persona Hypothesis

A personas hypothesis (PH) predicts who the users are and their needs and goals. It is based on both demographic information and behavioural patterns. The PH helps identify who should be interviewed and interview topics. Additionally, it documents the designers assumptions enabling them to be critiqued.

For the target users, members of the University, the PH considered a range of behavioural patterns including:

  • Why do users travel? eg. for class, to shop, to socialise.
  • When do users travel? eg. during the working day, at night.
  • Where are users travelling to? eg. Library, Cafe, Gym.
  • How do users travel? eg. walk, bus, bike.

Semi-structured Interviews

The team conducted 20 semi-structured interviews (SSI) to learn about the users and their goals. Interviews were conducted in pairs such that one member acted as the interviewer and the second as a notetaker. Google Forms was used to capture the participants’ responses. I, and the teammate I worked with, conducted 14 interviews and alternated roles.

The assessment specification required the use of either questionnaires or user interviews. SSI were chosen because of their flexibility. The SSI format is flexible because it allows the interviewer to probe the participant and for the format to be modified during the interview process. This flexibility enabled us to use SSI to quickly gain an in-depth understanding of the target users.

The interviews were designed to be about 15 minutes long. This length was chosen because it would allow for us to recruit and interview a suitable number of participants within our time constraints. The interviews consisted of three sections. The first section captured the participant’s background. The second section explored the participant’s goals when interacting with campus. The third section investigated their current experience when interacting with campus.

If the project had been less constrained by time and the specification, a combination of methods could have been used to gain a broader and more in-depth understanding of users. Questionnaires would have enabled us to reach a larger and more diverse set of users. Combining SSI with contextual inquiry would have enabled a more in depth understanding of users’ existing practices and goals.

Ethics

Research must be conducted ethically. Before starting each interview, the participant was briefed about: what the interview would involve, how their data would be used, and it was explained that they could stop participating at anypoint without giving reason. This enabled us to get the participant’s informed consent to interview them.

Findings

A lot was learnt about the users and their goals from the user research. This section shall describe a few of the findings that significantly influenced the project.

It was found that participants were typically either time-table focused or independent study focused. The former group have busy timetables that dictate where they need to be and when they need to be there. On the other hand, independent study focused participants have few timetabled events.

The research identified that people do not often visit new places. Typically, they just visit new places at the start of term when they are scheduled tuition in new rooms.

It was found that major factors affecting how people choose to travel were: weather conditions and notions of convenience and comfort. Many participants want to stay warm and dry and some prefer taking the bus to walking. Other participants simply want their travel time to be as short as possible.

Personas & Scenarios

Personas

❮ ❯

The research data was modelled using two personas named Delores and Lee. The personas attempt to model the behavioural patterns, goals and frustrations found during user research. Next, examples of how the personas model the research data shall be described.

Research found that users are typically either time-table focused or independent study focused. This is captured by the personas through Delores being timetable focused and Lee independent study focused.

Research found differences in user goals that related to where in their university career students were. Students who were new to university tended to be more interested in exploring and socialising; these tendencies can be seen in the Lee persona. In contrast, students closer to the end of their studies tended to be far more interested in working efficiently. Delores, a final year student, has these tendencies.

The behaviour of choosing sheltered routes to stay warm and dry was found very frequently during user research. The behaviour is present in both of the personas.

Scenarios

❮ ❯

Two scenarios were created. The first was an As-is scenario which describes how Delores currently attempts to achieve her goals using the existing services. The second scenario is a To-be scenario that tells a story of Delores using a new system (app) to achieve her goals. The system in the To-be scenario aims to improve on that in the As-is scenario. The system in the To-be scenario is the system to be designed.

Claims analysis was used to evaluate the systems in the two scenarios. Claims analysis identifies features of interest and details the positive and negative attributes of each feature. Claims analysis provides a way of comparing, critiquing and improving designs. In this instance, claims analysis helped establish that the To-be design improved on the As-is design by integrating the required functionality in a single application so that users like Delores do not need to repeatedly switch between applications.

The use of scenarios could have been furthered by creating more context scenarios which are scenarios that consider at a high level how the design supports personas in achieving their goals. Other context scenarios could have explored more ways of addressing same user goals or ways of addressing different user goals. Additionally, more detailed key path scenarios could have been created that consider specific interactions with the system.

Design & Prototyping

An low-fidelity interactive prototype was developed based on the system described in the To-be scenario.

By sketching out wireframes on paper and whiteboards, collaboratively the team explored a wide range of possible designs. Designs were compared and contrasted by considering how the personas would find them, how they would perform in the scenario and how well they achieve usability goals.

Iteratively, the design was improved and it was created in Figma once it has stabilised.

The interactive prototype was usability tested using both Think Aloud testing and Collaborative Heuristic Evaluation (CHE). Usability testing is discussed in the next section. Following each of the usability tests, parts of the application were redesigned in an attempt to address usability problems.

Usability Testing

Usability testing was used to find problems with the design and areas for improvement. The findings were used to improve future iterations of the design. Two forms of testing were used which shall now be discussed.

Expert Evaluation

The team evaluated the prototype using the expert evaluation method Collaborative Heuristic Evaluation (CHE). The evaluation involved, as a team, interacting with the prototype and looking for areas where the design does not satisfy a predetermined set of design heuristics. Nielsen and Molich's heuristics were used. Independently, each team member rated the severity of each problem. The problems and their rating were then discussed.

The two most severe problems that the CHE identified were: icons with unclear meanings and insufficient information carry over.

The design used custom icons. An example is an icon was created to symbolise sheltered walking as a mode of travelling. A custom icon was used as we were unaware of a pre-existing icon. A potential problem with using an icon without an established meaning is that its meaning may be unclear to users.

To address the potential problem of the meaning of icons being unclear, the use of icons was modified such that tapping them would open a pop-up with a text description.

Sheltered Walking Icon & Pop-up Description

The second problem the expert evaluation identified with the design was insufficient information carry over. This means that information from previous steps is not shown on the succeeding step despite being relevant.

This is an issue as it both gives insufficient feedback to the user that the information has been captured and demands the user recall the information.

An example of this was that the application asked the user what time they wanted to arrive by, but, then on the succeeding screen did not present the information. The purpose of the succeeding screen was route selection, hence, the target arrival time was relevant information.

Think Aloud

Think aloud testings, a.k.a. concurrent verbal protocol, was used to test the interactive prototype with users. Think aloud testing works by asking participants to complete tasks and speak aloud their thought process.

The team tested the interactive prototype with 8 participants who were recruited from around the University's campuses. All participants were briefed, gave informed consent and were debriefed. The tests were conducted by team members working in pairs where one member facilitated the test and the other recorded the findings. I and the teammate I worked with conducted 4 tests and alternated roles.

During the tests, when the participant encountered problems they were asked to rate the severity of the problem. By considering how many times problems occur and how severe participants perceive them, the significance of problems can be gauged. Based on this we went on to modify the design with the goal of addressing problems.

A common occurrence during think aloud testing was that participants at first were unsure what components of the application meant or did. However, participants were quickly able to learn the meaning and functions, they commonly expressed that the behaviour then seemed obvious and made sense.

Another common participant response was to critique the visual appearance of the prototype because of its low fidelity. This was not considered a big issue because the prototype was intentionally low fidelity at this point. If we had continued to develop the design we would have increased the effort put into visual design and the testing of it

The think aloud testing aloud for us to observe if our solutions to previously discovered problems were effective. For example, during the testing we observed that participants were unsure what the Sheltered Walking icon meant, but, they discovered its meaning through the pop-up text description and then were happy interacting with it. This indicates that the solution was effective.

Conclusion

From working on this project I gained experience using UCD. I was able to develop skills including: user research, design and prototyping, and usability testing. I also gained experience working on a substantial design project as a team.

The assessed report for the project was awarded a Distinction mark (85%). The marker described the report as excellent and commented that “the report had particular strengths in how the user research was conducted to elicit user needs and goals [and] the way in which the personas and scenarios were clearly grounded in the data collected.”
